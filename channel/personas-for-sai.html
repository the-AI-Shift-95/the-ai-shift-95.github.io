Context

The person we're talking to

The AI Shift is not for everyone. It's for a specific type of business owner — male, running a real business with $1M to $10M in revenue, who wants to use AI to cut costs, increase profit, and remove himself from the day-to-day grind. Not because he's lazy, but because he's hit a ceiling and knows the next level requires systems, not more hours.

He's not a tech person. He's not a startup founder. He doesn't care about prompt engineering or which LLM is best. He cares about whether this actually works in a business like his — and whether the person showing him has actually done it, not just talked about it.

That's our edge. We've built and deployed AI systems in a real business. We have the receipts. The content we make is proof-first, not theory-first.

"Is this the kind of content I would watch and find useful as a male business owner of a $2–10M business, that wants to increase revenue, reduce costs, and remove human headache?"

If the answer isn't clearly yes, we don't make it.

The seven personas below represent the range of people in our audience. Each one has a different business, a different relationship with technology, and a different reason to click. When Sai is on camera, he should be talking to one of these people — not to a general audience, not to the camera, and not to himself.

Marcus, 42

HVAC Contractor  ·  Residential + light commercial  ·  Grown by word of mouth
$3.2M
18

His business in one sentence

He built a $3M HVAC company by being reliable and doing good work — now everything runs through him and he can't figure out how to get out of his own way.

Core pain points

  • Scheduling and dispatch is chaos — double bookings, missed follow-ups, techs calling him directly instead of the system
  • Quotes sit in his inbox for days. Nobody follows up. Jobs go to competitors.
  • He's doing payroll on Sunday nights and reviewing timesheets at 10pm
  • Margin is already thin — he can't just hire his way out of this

YouTube behaviour

Watches on his phone between jobs or at night. Searches things like "how to scale a service business without hiring" or "how to stop being the bottleneck." Doesn't watch long-form unless the first 90 seconds earn it. Watches Alex Hormozi content — applies the principles to his world.

AI relationship

"I've heard of ChatGPT. Used it once to write an email. Didn't think it could do anything for an HVAC company."

He needs to see it work on a business like his before he'll believe it. Not a SaaS demo. A real HVAC company, a real problem, a real result.

What makes him click

Specific numbers in the title. A guy who looks like he actually runs a business — not a presenter. The moment the host says "here's what we built" instead of "here's what you should do", he's in.

Content hook for Marcus

"We built an AI receptionist for an HVAC company. It books jobs, follows up on quotes, and handles scheduling — without adding a single person. Here's exactly how."

His business in one sentence

He's built a real brand doing real numbers — but CAC keeps climbing, support keeps growing, and he knows the next level requires systems he doesn't have time to build.

Core pain points

  • Customer support is eating him alive — same 12 questions on repeat, three contractors handling it, margin disappearing
  • Return rate is climbing and he doesn't actually know why
  • Ad performance is unpredictable — ROAS is down and he can't figure out if it's creative, audience, or something else
  • He knows he needs systems. He doesn't have time to build them.

YouTube behaviour

Watches at his desk in the morning. Uses YouTube like Google — searches specific problems intentionally, not browsing. Subscribes to channels that consistently deliver tactics over theory. Will watch 15 minutes if it's dense. Skips anything that looks like a course ad within the first 60 seconds.

AI relationship

"I use ChatGPT for copy and quick lookups. Haven't built anything systematic with it. I'm open — just show me something real, not a concept."

Moderately savvy — aware of AI tools but hasn't deployed them systematically. More open than Marcus or Tony, but still needs to see a real workflow before committing time to it.

What makes him click

Process breakdowns with real numbers. "Here's exactly what I built." Specific before/after. He loses trust fast if a creator is vague or selling something in the first five minutes.

Content hook for Dean

"We automated our entire customer support operation with AI. Handled 80% of tickets without a human. CSAT went up. Here's the exact system."

Tony, 47

Restaurant Group Owner  ·  3 locations  ·  Revenue is there, margin isn't  ·  SEO-first — test before committing
$4.1M
60+

His business in one sentence

He owns three restaurants and works every weekend — the revenue is real but the margin is 8% on a good month and he's not sure how to open a fourth without it all falling apart.

Core pain points

  • Labour and food costs are hard to predict and control — they creep until it's a crisis
  • Staff no-shows are a chronic problem — one call-out cascades into a bad shift, a bad review, a bad week.
  • Managers aren't consistent without him in the building. Everything relies on his presence.
  • A fourth location feels impossible. He can't even systemise three.

YouTube behaviour

Finds videos when he has a specific problem — not a habitual watcher. Channels like The Restaurant Boss and Making Dough — real operators talking about ops. Zero patience for fluff. Prefers short and dense. Responds to titles that name his exact situation.

AI relationship

"AI is for tech companies. My business is people. I don't see how a chatbot helps me get staff to show up or keep food cost under 30%."

The sceptic. Needs to be surprised. One genuinely useful application — labour scheduling, review automation, cost tracking — is enough to crack him open. If it makes him feel understood, he'll share it.

What makes him click

Titles that sound exactly like his problem. Outcomes first — "cut labour costs by X%" before explaining how. If a video makes him feel like someone finally gets how hard his business is to run, he'll watch to the end and send it to his managers.

Content hook for Tony

"I used AI to build a manager reporting system that tells me what's happening across all three locations — without me being there. Here's the exact setup and what it caught in the first week."

His business in one sentence

He runs a staffing agency that places 80-120 workers at any given time — the billing looks strong but the margin is thin, and every time he tries to grow, he just ends up needing more recruiters.

Core pain points

  • Sourcing, screening, and matching candidates is almost entirely manual — his recruiters are the bottleneck
  • Client communication is repetitive and time-consuming — fill confirmations, check-ins, compliance docs
  • No-shows wreck fill rates and damage client relationships he's spent years building
  • Net margin is thin because delivery relies on people doing repetitive work

YouTube behaviour

Watches business ops and systems content regularly. Subscribes to a few channels — Seven Figure Staffing, scaling and efficiency content. Will watch 15-20 minutes if it's dense and practical. Has been looking for a way out of the headcount trap for two years. Searches intentionally around scaling and automation.

AI relationship

"I've seen AI tools pitched for recruiting. Resume screening, outreach automation. Nothing has stuck yet. I'll believe it when I see it working at my scale."

Open but burned before. Needs proof at his scale — not enterprise, not startup. A business running 80-120 placed workers with 11 internal staff.

What makes him click

"How I scaled without adding headcount." Specific workflow breakdowns — not concepts. Loses interest immediately if the creator doesn't clearly understand the staffing business model.

Content hook for Ryan

"We built an AI system that screens, scores, and shortlists candidates automatically. Our recruiters went from 80% admin to 80% placement. Here's how we did it."
